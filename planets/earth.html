Earth Details

Composition

Earth is the densest planet in the Solar System, consisting of a rocky mantle, liquid outer core, and solid inner core. The surface is approximately 71% water and 29% land.

Atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere is composed of 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, with trace amounts of other gases. This unique composition supports life and regulates temperature.

Magnetic Field

Earth generates a powerful magnetic field from its liquid outer core, protecting the planet from harmful solar radiation and solar wind.

Plate Tectonics

Earth is the only known planet with active plate tectonics, which drive mountain formation, earthquakes, volcanic activity, and continent movement over geological time scales.

Water Cycle

Earth's unique water cycle involves evaporation, condensation, and precipitation, creating rivers, oceans, glaciers, and weather patterns that sustain life.

Climate & Weather

Earth experiences diverse climate zones from polar ice caps to tropical rainforests, driven by atmospheric circulation and ocean currents.

Biosphere

Earth is the only known planet with life, hosting millions of species in complex ecosystems from deep ocean trenches to high mountain peaks.

Blue Color

Earth's distinctive blue appearance comes from the scattering of sunlight in our atmosphere and the vast oceans that cover most of its surface.

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite and the fifth largest moon in the Solar System. It plays a crucial role in stabilizing Earth's axial tilt (23.5 degrees) and creating ocean tides through gravitational pull.

The Moon formed approximately 4.5 billion years ago, likely from the debris of a giant impact between Earth and a Mars-sized body called Theia. This impact theory explains the Moon's composition and similar isotopic ratios to Earth's mantle.

The Moon's surface is covered with craters from billions of years of meteorite impacts, dark maria formed by ancient volcanic lava flows, and bright highlands composed of lighter-colored rocks. The same side always faces Earth due to tidal locking.

Earth Exploration

Early Humanity (Prehistory)

Humanity emerged on Earth around 300,000 years ago, developing tools, agriculture, civilization, and eventually scientific understanding of our planet and its place in the cosmos.

Age of Discovery (15th-17th centuries)

European explorers circumnavigated Earth, proving it was round and mapping its continents, oceans, and diverse cultures, leading to global trade and cultural exchange.

Scientific Revolution (17th-19th centuries)

Scientists like Newton, Darwin, and others developed fundamental understanding of Earth's physics, geology, biology, and place in the Solar System.

Space Age (1957-Present)

From Sputnik to the International Space Station, humanity has viewed Earth from space, revealing its beauty, fragility, and the unified nature of our planetary home.

Satellite Era (1960s-Present)

Thousands of satellites monitor Earth's weather, climate, resources, and human activities, providing unprecedented data for understanding and managing our planet.

Environmental Awareness (1970s-Present)

Growing understanding of climate change, biodiversity loss, and environmental challenges has driven global efforts to protect Earth's ecosystems for future generations.

Digital Earth (21st Century)

GPS, internet, AI, and big data technologies enable detailed monitoring, modeling, and management of Earth's systems at unprecedented scales.
