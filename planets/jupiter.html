Jupiter Details

Composition

Jupiter is the largest planet in our Solar System, a gas giant composed primarily of hydrogen and helium. It may have a rocky core, but most of its volume consists of metallic hydrogen and liquid hydrogen.

Great Red Spot

The Great Red Spot is a massive storm that has been raging for at least 350 years. This anticyclonic storm is larger than Earth and rotates counterclockwise with wind speeds exceeding 400 km/h.

Bands and Zones

Jupiter's distinctive banded appearance comes from alternating cloud layers called zones (lighter, rising clouds) and belts (darker, sinking clouds). These create complex weather patterns with winds reaching 500 km/h.

Magnetic Field

Jupiter has the strongest magnetic field of any planet, about 20,000 times stronger than Earth's. This creates powerful radiation belts and spectacular aurora at its poles.

Radiation Belts

Jupiter's intense magnetic field traps charged particles, creating dangerous radiation belts that would be lethal to unprotected spacecraft and humans.

Internal Heat

Jupiter generates more heat than it receives from the Sun, likely from the gradual compression of its massive interior through Kelvin-Helmholtz contraction.

Ring System

Jupiter has a faint ring system composed of dust particles ejected from its moons during meteorite impacts. The rings were discovered in 1979 by the Voyager 1 spacecraft.

The four largest moons, discovered by Galileo in 1610, are called the Galilean moons. Io is the most volcanically active body in the Solar System. Europa likely has a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust. Ganymede is the largest moon in the Solar System and has its own magnetic field. Callisto has the oldest and most cratered surface of any known object in the Solar System.

Jupiter Exploration

Early Observations (Ancient Times)

Jupiter has been known since ancient times as one of the brightest "wandering stars" in the night sky. Its name comes from the king of the Roman gods.

Galileo's Discovery (1610)

Galileo Galilei discovered Jupiter's four largest moons using his telescope, providing crucial evidence for the Copernican heliocentric model of the Solar System.

Pioneer Missions (1973-1974)

Pioneer 10 and 11 were the first spacecraft to fly by Jupiter, providing close-up images and data about Jupiter's intense radiation environment and magnetic field.

Voyager Missions (1979)

Voyager 1 and 2 conducted detailed studies of Jupiter and its moons, discovering active volcanoes on Io and Jupiter's faint ring system.

Galileo Orbiter (1995-2003)

NASA's Galileo spacecraft was the first to orbit Jupiter, conducting extensive studies of the planet and its moons, including dropping a probe into Jupiter's atmosphere.

Juno Mission (2016-Present)

NASA's Juno spacecraft is currently studying Jupiter's composition, gravity field, magnetic field, and polar magnetosphere, providing unprecedented insights into the planet's formation and evolution.

Future Missions

Europa Clipper (planned 2024) will study Jupiter's moon Europa for potential habitability, while JUICE (ESA) will explore Jupiter and its icy moons.
