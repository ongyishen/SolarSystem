Venus Details

Composition

Venus is often called Earth's "sister planet" due to their similar size and mass. However, its surface and atmospheric conditions are drastically different, making it one of the most inhospitable places in the Solar System.

Extreme Greenhouse Effect

Venus has the strongest greenhouse effect in the Solar System. Its thick atmosphere of carbon dioxide traps heat, creating surface temperatures of 462¬∞C (864¬∞F) - hot enough to melt lead.

Retrograde Rotation

Venus rotates backwards compared to most planets, in a direction opposite to its orbit. A Venusian day is longer than its year, taking 243 Earth days to complete one rotation.

Surface Features

Venus features vast volcanic plains, mountain ranges, and thousands of volcanoes. The surface is relatively young geologically, suggesting widespread volcanic resurfacing in the recent past.

Atmospheric Pressure

The atmospheric pressure on Venus' surface is 92 times that of Earth - equivalent to being 900 meters underwater in Earth's oceans. This pressure would crush unprotected spacecraft.

Sulfuric Acid Clouds

Venus is surrounded by thick clouds of sulfuric acid that reflect sunlight, making it the brightest object in Earth's night sky after the Moon. These clouds create a perpetual yellowish haze.

Volcanic Activity

Recent evidence suggests Venus may still be volcanically active, with lava flows and possible ongoing eruptions. This activity contributes to its young surface age.

No Natural Moons

Venus does not have any natural satellites or moons. Like Mercury, its proximity to the Sun and relatively slow rotation make gravitational capture of moons difficult.

The gravitational influence of the Sun and Venus's thick atmosphere would prevent any moon from maintaining a stable orbit around the planet.

Venus's unusual retrograde rotation also complicates the dynamics that would allow for stable moon orbits.

Venus Exploration

Early Observations (Ancient Times)

Venus has been observed since ancient times and is known as the "Morning Star" and "Evening Star" due to its brightness and visibility near sunrise and sunset.

Venera Missions (1961-1983)

The Soviet Union's Venera program achieved many firsts, including the first successful landing on another planet (Venera 7 in 1970) and the first color images from Venus' surface.

Magellan (1990-1994)

NASA's Magellan spacecraft used radar to map Venus' surface through its thick clouds, revealing detailed topography and evidence of past volcanic activity.

Express (2005-Present)

ESA's Venus Express orbiter studied Venus' atmosphere, discovering evidence for recent volcanic activity and mysterious atmospheric phenomena.

Akatsuki (2015-Present)

Japan's Akatsuki spacecraft is studying Venus' atmosphere and weather patterns, including enigmatic cloud movements and atmospheric superrotation.

Future Missions

Upcoming missions include NASA's VERITAS and DAVINCI+, and ESA's EnVision, all planned for the late 2020s to early 2030s, to study Venus' surface and atmosphere in unprecedented detail.
